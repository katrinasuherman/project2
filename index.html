Proposition: Only a global event like COVID-19 could bring down Southern Europe’s manufacturing methane emissions.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions, Scores, and Rationale:

  • Bold red line and large downward marker (Score: -1.5) — Saturated red color (`#D73027`) emphasizes 2020 drop visually and emotionally.
  • Shaded COVID year (Score: -1.0) — Highlighting 2019.5–2020.5 isolates the pandemic period and guides interpretation.
  • “Pandemic-Induced Drop” annotation box (Score: -1.0) — Text framing supports pandemic causality subtly without numbers.
  • Pre-COVID baseline band (Score: -1.0) — Shows a "normal" range that makes 2020 appear exceptional.
  • Truncated y-axis (Score: -2.0) — Compresses vertical scale to visually exaggerate the magnitude of the dip.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions, Scores, and Rationale:

  • Expanded y-axis (4 to 7) (Score: -1.5) — Flattens year-to-year fluctuations and makes the COVID dip seem insignificant.
  • Added linear regression trend line (Score: -1.0) — Emphasizes long-term gradual decline rather than short-term disruption.
  • Muted raw data line and small square markers (Score: -1.0) — Lowers the visual weight of yearly variability, highlighting only trend.
  • Removed gridlines (Score: 0) — Simplifies the visual background without misleading about data.
  • Narrative framing in subtitle (Score: -0.5) — Guides viewer’s interpretation toward stability across the COVID period.

Final Reflection

This project taught me how powerful subtle design choices can be in shaping a viewer’s perception of a dataset without altering the actual numbers. It was straightforward to create a persuasive visualization emphasizing the 2020 drop because there were many visual levers available (color, annotation, axis scaling). The more difficult part was designing an opposing view that downplayed the same drop while remaining visually clean and plausible.

I was surprised by how much small details — such as gridlines, marker size, or line opacity — could steer interpretation. Ethical analysis and visualization, in my view, involves using design choices intentionally while being transparent about what is being emphasized. Acceptable persuasive choices guide the reader’s attention but do not fabricate or mislead about the core data itself. Misleading choices, in contrast, fundamentally obscure, delete, or falsify important information. Through this assignment, I now see how ethical visualization lies in that delicate balance between expression and distortion.
